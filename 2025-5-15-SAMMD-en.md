## Overview

**Maximum mean discrepancy (MMD)** [(Gretton et al., 2012a)](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/volume13/gretton12a/gretton12a.pdf) measures the closeness of two distributions $\mathbb{P}$ and $\mathbb{Q}$:

$$\text{MMD}(\mathbb{P},\mathbb{Q}; \mathcal{F}) := \sup\_{f\in\mathcal{F}}|\mathbb{E}[f(X)]-\mathbb{E}[f(Y)]|$$

where $\mathcal{F}$ is the set of all continuous functions.

> [!NOTE]
> **Continuous Functions**
>
> A function $f : \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is called continuous if a "small change in input produces only a small change in output."
>
> By measure theory (Portmanteau Theorem), the family of continuous functions is rich enough to distinguish two different probability distributions. For $\mathbb{P} \ne \mathbb{Q}$, there necessarily exists some continuous function $f$ whose expectations under $\mathbb{P}$ and $\mathbb{Q}$ differ.

To obtain an analytic solution for the above formula, [(Gretton et al., 2012a)](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/volume13/gretton12a/gretton12a.pdf) restricts $\mathcal{F}$ to the unit ball of a _reproducing kernel Hilbert space_ $\mathcal{H}\\_{k}$ (i.e., $\|f\|\\_{\mathcal{H}\\_{k}}\le1$). Then MMD can be expressed as the norm distance of _kernel mean embeddings_:

$$\begin{aligned} \text{MMD}(\mathbb{P}, \mathbb{Q};\mathcal{H}\_k) &:= \sup\_{f\in\mathcal{H}, \|f\|_{\mathcal{H}\_k} \le 1} |\mathbb{E}[f(X)] - \mathbb{E}[f(Y)]| \\ &= \|\mu\_\mathbb{P}-\mu\_\mathbb{Q}\|_{\mathcal{H}\_k} \end{aligned}$$

where $k(\cdot,\cdot)$ is a bounded kernel function (e.g., Gaussian kernel).

In adversarial example detection experiments, [(Carlini & Wagner, 2017a)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/3128572.3140444) found that the MMD method cannot effectively identify adversarial examples. Their experiments showed that the MMD test yields a $p$-value above a given threshold, yet the samples themselves belong to clean and adversarial examples respectively. In other words, the MMD test considered the two samples to share the same distribution.

Motivated by this, [(Gao et al., 2020)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2010.11415) proposed **semantic-aware MMD (SAMMD)**, which addresses the problem by replacing the Gaussian kernel with a semantic-aware deep kernel.

### Mathematical Foundations of Two-Sample Testing

*   **Characteristic Kernel** property: If the kernel $k$ is characteristic, then $\text{MMD}\left(P,Q\right)=0\Leftrightarrow P=Q$. As long as MMD is significantly larger than 0, we can conclude that the two samples come from different distributions.

In MMD testing, given two sets of observations drawn from $\mathbb{P}$ and $\mathbb{Q}$, we need to test these two samples to determine whether they originate from the same distribution. The procedure is: first, estimate the MMD value from the two samples; then, compute the $p$-value corresponding to the MMD value [(Sutherland et al., 2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.04488). If the resulting $p$-value exceeds the threshold $\alpha$, we conclude that the two samples are from the same distribution.

**Two-sample test.** Let $\mathcal{X} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ and $\mathbb{P}$, $\mathbb{Q}$ be Borel probability measures on $\mathcal{X}$. Given i.i.d samples $S\_X = \{\mathbf{x}\_i\}^n\_{i=1} \sim \mathbb{P}^n$ and $S\_Y = \{\mathbf{y}\_j\}^m\_{j=1} \sim \mathbb{Q}^m$, in the two-sample test problem, we aim to determine if $S\_{X}$ and $S\_{Y}$ come from the same distribution, i.e. if $\mathbb{P}=\mathbb{Q}$.

**Estimation of MMD**. We use the $U$-statistic to obtain an unbiased estimate of $\text{MMD}^{^2}$, which has near-minimal variance among unbiased estimators [(Gretton et al., 2012a)](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/volume13/gretton12a/gretton12a.pdf):

$$\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2\_{u}(S\_X, S\_Y; k) = \frac{1}{n(n-1)}\sum\_{i\ne j}H\_{ij},$$
$$H\_{ij} = k(\bm{x}\_i, \bm{x}\_j) + k(\bm{y}\_i, \bm{y}\_j) - k(\bm{x}\_i, \bm{y}\_j)-k(\bm{y}\_i, \bm{x}\_j),$$

where $\bm{x}\_i, \bm{x}\_j \in S\_X$ and $\bm{y}\_i, \bm{y}\_j \in S\_Y$.

**Non-parametric two-sample tests**. No assumptions are made about $\mathbb{P}$ and $\mathbb{Q}$ belonging to any pre-specified parametric family. The construction of the test statistic and the specific mathematical form of the distribution are entirely data-driven [(Liu et al., 2020b)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v119/liu20m.html).

### Do Adversarial and Clean Samples Belong to Different Distributions? [Gao et al., 2020](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2010.11415)

[(Gao et al., 2020)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2010.11415) first answers affirmatively: yes! Adversarial and clean samples belong to different distributions. But why the paradox in [(Carlini & Wagner, 2017a)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/abs/10.1145/3128572.3140444)? [(Gao et al., 2020)](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2010.11415) argues that earlier experiments overlooked three key factors.

1.  Limited expressiveness of the Gaussian kernel
    1.  It cannot adequately measure similarity between two high-dimensional samples (e.g., images) [(Wenliang et al., 2019)](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v97/wenliang19a.html);
    2.  The Gaussian kernel **treats all data dimensions uniformly**, instead of focusing on regions where the two distributions differ;
    3.  Resulting problems:
        1.  A large number of samples is required to distinguish the two distributions;
        2.  For complex data (images), test power is severely limited;
    4.  The paper's solution:
        1.  Propose a **semantic-aware deep kernel** to replace the Gaussian kernel, leveraging a trained classifier to extract semantic features for measuring sample similarity.
    5.  **Conclusion:** The MMD-G test (using a Gaussian kernel) has substantially lower test power than MMD-D (deep kernel) and MMD-O (optimized Gaussian kernel).

2.  Neglect of kernel parameter optimization
    1.  For the MMD-G test, its power is closely tied to the **bandwidth** choice of the Gaussian kernel. Neglecting bandwidth optimization leads to a significant drop in test power ([Gretton et al., 2012a](https://www.jmlr.org/papers/volume13/gretton12a/gretton12a.pdf); [Sutherland et al., 2017](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.04488));
    2.  Even with optimized bandwidth $\sigma$, the Gaussian kernel still has limited expressiveness for complex distributions.
    3.  The paper's solution: The paper analyzes the asymptotic statistical properties of SAMMD, derives an approximate test power, and optimizes the deep kernel parameters by maximizing test power:

> [!NOTE]
> **Asymptotic Properties of SAMMD**
>
> Proposition 2 of Liu et al., 2020 and Theorem 1 of Gao et al., 2021 state that under the alternative hypothesis $H\_1$, when the sample size is sufficiently large, the $U$-statistic estimator of MMD/SAMMD satisfies **asymptotic normality**:
>
> $$\sqrt{n}(\widehat{\text{MMD}}^2\_u - \text{MMD}^2) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma\_{H\_1}^2)$$
>
> That is, when $n$ is large, the computed estimate clusters around the true value $\text{MMD}^2$ following a normal distribution, with fluctuation controlled by $\sigma\_{H\_1}/\sqrt{n}$.

3.  Adversarial samples may be non-IID. This situation directly breaks the fundamental assumptions of MMD.
    1.  When dependencies exist between observations, the Type I Error of the standard MMD test exceeds the prescribed threshold $\alpha$, rendering the test invalid.
    2.  **Solution**: Use the **wild bootstrap** method to resample SAMMD values, ensuring correct $p$-values under both non-IID and IID scenarios.
#### Summary

The three factors above are interconnected:

*   Factors 1 & 2 lead to low detection rates;
*   Factor 3 directly causes the detection method itself to be invalid.

The corresponding three mitigations:

1.  Semantic-aware deep kernel;
2.  Maximizing test power based on asymptotic statistics;
3.  Wild bootstrap resampling.
